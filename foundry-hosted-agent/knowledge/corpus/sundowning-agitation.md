# Sundowning and late-day agitation

Many people with dementia become more confused, restless, or anxious in the late afternoon and
evening — a pattern often called "sundowning." It can include pacing, irritability, suspicion, or
wanting to leave.

## In the moment
- Stay calm and unhurried; your tone and body language set the mood.
- Reduce demands and stimulation: lower noise, turn on soft lighting before dusk to limit
  confusing shadows, and turn off busy television.
- Redirect to a simple, soothing activity — folding towels, a familiar song, a warm drink, or
  looking at photos.
- Validate feelings rather than arguing with beliefs. "You seem worried — I'm here with you."

## Common triggers to reduce
- Tiredness and too little daytime activity or daylight.
- Hunger, thirst, needing the toilet, or pain.
- Overstimulation, clutter, or too many people late in the day.
- Caffeine or a large meal late in the day.

## Prevention
- Keep a predictable daily rhythm with activity and daylight earlier in the day.
- Wind down gradually in the evening. Persistent, distressing agitation that does not settle is
  worth a clinician review, as it can have a treatable cause.
