# Nighttime wandering response

People living with dementia often wake and move around at night. Wandering is usually driven by
an unmet need or disorientation rather than intent, so the caregiver's first job is to stay calm
and keep the person safe.

## In the moment
- Approach slowly from the front and enter the person's line of sight before speaking. Do not
  approach from behind or startle them.
- Speak softly and use short, reassuring sentences. Use the person's name and a warm tone.
- Avoid arguing or correcting. If they believe they need to "go to work" or "find someone,"
  acknowledge the feeling and gently redirect.
- Guide them back toward bed once they are calm. Offer a hand rather than pulling.

## Check for unmet needs
Wandering is frequently caused by something fixable. Check for:
- Toileting needs or a wet garment.
- Thirst, hunger, or pain.
- Being too hot or too cold, or uncomfortable bedding.
- Noise, light, or an unfamiliar shadow causing confusion.

## After the episode
- Note the time and possible trigger so patterns can be spotted over days.
- If wandering happens most nights, consider a caregiver review of the evening routine, lighting,
  and daytime activity, which can reduce nighttime restlessness.
