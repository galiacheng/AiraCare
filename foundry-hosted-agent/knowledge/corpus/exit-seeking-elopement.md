# Exit-seeking and elopement risk

Exit-seeking is when a person with dementia tries to leave the home — opening doors, gathering
belongings, or heading for an exit. Leaving unsupervised (elopement) is dangerous, especially at
night or in cold weather, because the person can become lost or disoriented outdoors.

## Immediate response
- If a door was opened, quickly confirm the person's location before anything else.
- Secure exits calmly. Do not physically block or grab the person, which can escalate distress.
- Redirect gently: offer a warm drink, a familiar activity, or a walk to another room together.
- Reassure that they are safe and in the right place.

## Why it is higher risk
- Repeated nighttime exit-seeking is an elopement risk and warrants caregiver escalation, not
  just in-the-moment redirection.
- A single successful exit at night can lead to a fall, exposure, or getting lost, so prevention
  matters more than reaction.

## Reducing the risk over time
- Consider door chimes or sensors that alert a caregiver when an exit opens.
- Place locks out of the usual line of sight, and reduce visual cues that suggest leaving
  (coats, keys, shoes by the door).
- Ensure the person carries or wears identification. Address daytime boredom and restlessness,
  which often drive evening exit-seeking.
