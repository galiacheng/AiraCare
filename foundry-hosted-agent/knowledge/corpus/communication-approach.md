# Communicating with a person living with dementia

Good communication prevents much distress and many behavioural "problems." The goal is to
connect and reassure, not to correct.

## Approach
- Get to the person's eye level from the front, make gentle eye contact, and say who you are.
- Reduce background noise so they can focus on you.
- Use a calm, warm voice and an unhurried pace.

## What to say and how
- Keep it simple: one idea and one short sentence at a time.
- Ask one question at a time, and prefer yes/no or two-option choices over open-ended questions.
- Give time to respond — silence is fine; do not rush or finish their sentences.
- Use names rather than pronouns ("Here is your tea" rather than "Here is it").

## Avoid
- Arguing, quizzing ("Don't you remember?"), or correcting false beliefs, which causes distress.
- Talking about the person as if they are not there.

## When words are hard
- Read emotion and body language; respond to the feeling behind the words.
- Use gestures, a reassuring touch on the hand if welcomed, and a familiar routine to communicate
  safety even when language fails.
