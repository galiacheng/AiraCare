# When to escalate and emergency warning signs

Most daily-living events are handled at home with calm reassurance. Some signs mean it is time to
call for help. This note describes *when* to seek help — it does not replace clinical judgment or
local emergency guidance.

## Call emergency services now if the person has
- No response, difficulty breathing, or blue/grey lips.
- Chest pain, a suspected stroke (face drooping, arm weakness, slurred speech), or a seizure.
- A serious fall: possible broken bone, a head injury, or a head bump while on blood thinners.
- Heavy bleeding or a severe burn.

## Contact a caregiver or clinician promptly (urgent, not 911) for
- New or rapidly worsening confusion, drowsiness, or agitation — often a sign of infection,
  dehydration, pain, or a medication effect.
- A fever, repeated vomiting, or not drinking for a prolonged period.
- Refusing important medication repeatedly, or a suspected medication error.
- A new, sustained change in behaviour, mobility, or continence.

## The escalation ladder
For a high-risk situation, AiraCare uses a timed ladder — **family first, then community, then
emergency** — where an acknowledgement resolves a rung. Knowledge guidance helps a caregiver
decide *what to do*; it never triggers the ladder itself. If in doubt about immediate safety, err
toward seeking help.
